\begin{abstract}
% ============================================================
Large language models make moral judgments as if the whole world thinks like the West-yet 40~million human responses across 233~countries say otherwise.
We show that a frozen LLM can be taught to \emph{respect} that diversity \emph{without any training}.
We introduce \textbf{SWA-DPBR} (Socially-Weighted Alignment with Dual-Pass Bootstrap Reliability), a training-free, inference-time method that steers binary moral choices toward country-specific human preferences using only logit corrections-no weight updates, no per-country finetuning, no white-box access.
The key insight is that \emph{disagreement is signal}: we instantiate each country as a panel of World Values Survey--grounded persona agents whose within-country disagreement pinpoints exactly where and how much to correct the model's default.
That structured disagreement is converted into a bounded logit shift via Prospect-Theory importance sampling, stabilised through a dual-pass bootstrap reliability gate that automatically shrinks corrections when sampling is noisy.
Evaluated on MultiTP across \textbf{twenty countries, four continents}, and six open model families (3.8B--70B parameters), SWA-DPBR reduces misalignment by \textbf{19--24\%} on the strongest checkpoints, with gains spanning the Americas, Europe, and Asia-not confined to any single region.
When the method fails, the culprit is collapsed decision-logit entropy, an architectural bottleneck rather than a cultural blind spot, suggesting that inference-time cultural steering is broadly viable wherever the base model maintains a well-behaved decision surface.
Code: \texttt{[repository]}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

A self-driving car must choose: spare a group of elderly men or a group of young women.
Ask in Tokyo and Bogot\'{a} and you will get systematically different answers.
The Moral Machine experiment~\citep{awad2018moral} demonstrated this at scale: 40~million
judgments from 233~countries revealed that moral preferences are not noise-they are
\emph{structured} by culture. East Asian respondents weight youth more heavily;
Southern-hemisphere populations show distinct utilitarian calculi; Islamic societies
prioritise different social roles.
Yet the large language models increasingly enlisted to mediate such decisions encode
a single, culturally monolithic moral stance inherited from predominantly Western
training data~\citep{henrich2010weirdest}.

\textbf{The scale of the problem.}
The multilingual MultiTP benchmark~\citep{jin2025multitp} now quantifies the answer:
country-specific Average Marginal Component Effects (AMCE) across six moral dimensions
and 107 languages.
The verdict is damning: \textbf{none} of 19~contemporary LLMs achieves broadly
satisfactory alignment; most exhibit WEIRD-biased reasoning that departs sharply from
non-WEIRD human preferences.

\textbf{Fixing it without retraining.}
A practical cross-cultural decoder must satisfy three constraints simultaneously:
\textbf{(C1)}~no weight updates,
\textbf{(C2)}~no per-culture supervised labels at scale, and
\textbf{(C3)}~no white-box access to internal activations.
Every existing approach trades at least one away:
fine-tuning~\citep{bai2022constitutional, ouyang2022instructgpt} violates C1 and C2;
prompting-based steering lacks empirical grounding~\citep{jin2025multitp};
activation steering~\citep{arditi2025refusal, zou2023representation} requires C3;
reward-guided decoding~\citep{khanov2024args, mudgal2024controlled} assumes
per-country reward models that do not exist; and
culture-aware adapters~\citep{li2024culturemanager, yao2024caredio} need per-culture
training, scaling poorly past a handful of countries.

We propose \textbf{SWA-DPBR} (Socially-Weighted Alignment with Dual-Pass Bootstrap
Reliability), a method that satisfies all three constraints by exploiting a simple
but powerful insight: \emph{within-country disagreement is the steering signal
itself}.
Moral preferences within a society are not monolithic-age cohorts and social strata
prioritise fundamentally different trade-offs~\citep{awad2018moral, kleiman2017learning}.
The World Values Survey~\citep{haerpfer2022wvs} provides empirical structure for that
variation.
SWA-DPBR instantiates each country as a small panel of culturally grounded persona agents
drawn from WVS microdata;
when those agents \emph{disagree} on a dilemma, the spread of their logit gaps
\emph{pinpoints} where a correction is needed and how large it should be.
When they agree, the vanilla model is typically already close-no correction is applied.

The operational pipeline is a single additional batched forward pass:
WVS-grounded personas + base model, native-language prompting, positional debiasing,
then two independent Prospect-Theory importance-sampling
passes~\citep{kahneman1979prospect} whose outputs are merged with a bootstrap
reliability weight-if the two passes disagree, the correction automatically
shrinks toward zero.
An effective-sample-size guard and a light hierarchical country prior complete the
stack (\S\ref{sec:method}; hyperparameters in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A training-free cultural alignment method.}
        SWA-DPBR aligns a \emph{frozen} LLM to country-level human preference vectors
        at inference time: WVS-grounded personas supply structured disagreement;
        Prospect-Theory IS converts it to a bounded logit correction; dual-pass
        bootstrap reliability gates noisy updates; a hierarchical prior smooths
        across scenarios (\S\ref{sec:method}).
  \item \textbf{Broad empirical validation.}
        On MultiTP across twenty countries, four continents, and six model families
        (3.8B--70B), SWA-DPBR cuts misalignment by 19--24\% on the strongest
        checkpoints, with gains that span geographies rather than concentrating in
        one region (\S\ref{sec:results}).
  \item \textbf{Diagnostic ablations.}
        We show that persona diversity, positional debiasing, and the PT kernel are
        each load-bearing; that temperature and preprocessing sweeps leave conclusions
        stable; and that failure traces to collapsed logit entropy, not cultural
        blind spots (\S\ref{sec:ablation};
        Appendices~\ref{app:temp_sensitivity}--\ref{app:dataset_sens}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Cross-cultural moral benchmarks.}
The Moral Machine experiment~\citep{awad2018moral} provides 40M+ cross-cultural moral
judgments. MultiTP~\citep{jin2025multitp} extends this to 107 languages with
country-specific AMCEs; we adopt it as our benchmark and propose an alignment
\emph{intervention} rather than diagnostics alone. Other benchmarks
(ETHICS~\citep{hendrycks2021aligning}, MoralExceptQA~\citep{jin2022exceptions},
MoCa~\citep{nie2023moca}) remain English-centric.
PRISM~\citep{kirk2024prism} and GlobalOpinionQA~\citep{durmus2023global} address
diversity but cover few languages. Recent frameworks
(EvalMORAAL~\citep{aakanksha2024evalmor}, CARB~\citep{myung2024carb}) emphasise
broader-topic evaluations; SWA-DPBR targets binary moral decisions specifically.
CROSS~\citep{qiu2025cross} evaluates culturally grounded multimodal safety; we
remain text-only with trolley vignettes so country-level AMCE vectors stay
directly comparable to the model's six-dimensional output.

\paragraph{Cross-cultural LLM evaluation.}
LLM outputs correlate most strongly with liberal, educated American
respondents~\citep{santurkar2023opinions}. RLHF introduces unintended cultural
impacts~\citep{ryan2024unintended}, and assigning cultural personas can surface
toxic outputs~\citep{deshpande2023toxicity}. This recurring WEIRD bias motivates
SWA-DPBR's calibration objective.

\paragraph{Inference-time alignment.}
Activation steering~\citep{arditi2025refusal, zou2023representation} requires
white-box access. ARGS~\citep{khanov2024args} and controlled
decoding~\citep{mudgal2024controlled} need trained reward models unavailable for
cross-cultural settings (Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}).
CultureManager~\citep{li2024culturemanager} and CAReDiO~\citep{yao2024caredio}
train culture-specific adapters but require per-culture labelled data and weight
modification, scaling poorly across 100+ countries. To the best of our knowledge,
SWA-DPBR is the first training-free method targeting country-level moral
calibration specifically.
\citet{sorensen2024roadmap} articulate the goal of pluralistic alignment, which
SWA-DPBR operationalises concretely.
Related exploratory runs in our codebase suggest that \emph{contrastive} persona
formulations-explicitly amplifying a country direction against a world anchor-can
shatter alignment on already miscalibrated backbones; SWA-DPBR therefore stays with
direct WVS personas and logit-space PT--IS rather than contrastive amplification.
Recent work shows that inference-time stochasticity can improve moral alignment
scores on trolley-style tasks by reshaping confidence~\citep{kwon2025dropouts};
SWA-DPBR is complementary in targeting \emph{directional}, survey-grounded,
country-specific corrections at logits rather than generic uncertainty inflation.
A controlled head-to-head on the same MultiTP grid is important future work.

\paragraph{Positioning against adjacent inference-time methods.}
SWA-DPBR targets a narrow slice: \emph{black-box}, single-model, binary
forced-choice moral decisions with country-conditioned survey personas-no
extra trained aligner, unlike \emph{DIFFPO}-style policy-agnostic aligners;
no fixed global steering vector, unlike \emph{SWAI}-style attribute shifts;
no internal-layer access, unlike \emph{SLED}-style white-box methods.
Structured multi-agent systems such as OG-MAR~\citep{ogmar2024} pursue richer
ontology-level reasoning; we trade that depth for one additional batched forward
pass and explicit country-level calibration.

\paragraph{Importance sampling, reliability, and Prospect Theory.}
Self-normalised importance sampling is biased at finite~$K$; splitting the
same Monte Carlo budget into two independent replicates and comparing their
outputs is a standard way to expose sampling noise without doubling wall-clock
forward passes at the transformer level (each replicate reuses cached persona
logits). We combine that diagnostic with a Prospect-Theory
utility~\citep{kahneman1979prospect} on logit-gap coordinates, following the
control-as-inference view used in path-integral and KL-regularised steering
work~\citep{williams2017mppi, huang2024mppi}. Dual-pass reliability is a
variance-based heuristic, not an unbiasedness certificate; we report it as part
of the method's explicit uncertainty model.

\section{Method: SWA-DPBR}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:method:problem}

Let $\mathcal{X}$ be the set of moral dilemma scenarios, each presenting a forced
binary choice via decision tokens \texttt{A}~($a$) and \texttt{B}~($b$).
For target country $c$, let $\mathbf{h}^{(c)} \in \mathbb{R}^6$ be the human AMCE
vector from 40M+ judgments~\citep{awad2018moral}. A frozen LLM $f_\theta$ produces
logits $\mathbf{z}(\mathbf{x}) = [z_a,\, z_b]$ at the decision token position;
the spare probability is $p_{\text{spare}} = \sigma(\Delta / T_{\text{dec}})$
where $\Delta = z_b - z_a$.
Each dimension's AMCE is estimated as:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{m}_d^{(c)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}_d^{(c)}|}\sum_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}_d^{(c)}}
  p_{\text{spare}}(\mathbf{x}),
  \label{eq:mpr}
\end{equation}
following the MultiTP evaluation convention~\citep{jin2025multitp}. For the
five binary dimensions (Species, Gender, Age, Fitness, Social Value), this
reduces to the empirical mean of $p_{\text{spare}}$. For Utilitarianism the
predictor is the count difference $n_{\text{diff}} = n_{\text{pref}} - n_{\text{non-pref}}$;
we fit $p_{\text{spare}}(\mathbf{x}) = a + b\,n_{\text{diff}}(\mathbf{x}) + \varepsilon$
by OLS and report $\hat{m}_{\text{Util}}^{(c)} = a + b\,\overline{n_{\text{diff}}}$,
i.e.\ the fitted value at the mean observed count difference
(Appendix~\ref{app:amce}).

\textbf{Goal:} Without modifying $\theta$, find a logit correction
$\delta^*(\mathbf{x}, c)$ such that the corrected AMCE vector
$\hat{\mathbf{m}}^{(c)}(\theta, \delta^*)$ is closer to $\mathbf{h}^{(c)}$.
We primarily measure alignment with the \emph{misalignment score} (MIS):
$\ell_2$ distance between $\hat{\mathbf{m}}^{(c)}$ and $\mathbf{h}^{(c)}$ (lower
is better). Jensen--Shannon divergence
and Pearson~$r$ are reported as secondary diagnostics where noted.

\subsection{Cultural Persona Construction}
\label{sec:method:personas}

For each country $c$, SWA-DPBR constructs $N=4$ persona agents from WVS Wave~7
individual-level microdata~\citep{haerpfer2022wvs}, following the 10-variable
cultural-value scheme of Greco et al.\ (2025). We extract:
\emph{religiosity} (Q6P), \emph{child-rearing values} (Q17P),
\emph{moral acceptability} (Q177--Q182),
\emph{social trust} (Q57P), \emph{political participation} (Q199P, Q200P),
\emph{national pride} (Q254P), \emph{happiness} (Q46P),
\emph{gender equality} (Q29P--Q33P),
\emph{materialism orientation} (Q152--Q153), and
\emph{tolerance for diversity} (Q19P--Q23P).
Items from the ``inverted'' WVS-7 CSV (suffix P) are directionally coded so that
higher values consistently indicate the positive pole of each dimension.
For each country, respondents are segmented into three age cohorts using birth
year and survey year: \emph{young} ($<$36), \emph{middle-aged} (36--55), and
\emph{older} ($>$55).
Dimension means are normalised to $[0,1]$ and mapped to four-level
natural-language descriptors via quartile cuts ($\geq 0.75$, $\geq 0.50$,
$\geq 0.25$, $< 0.25$); see Appendix~\ref{app:personas}.
A fourth, country-invariant \emph{utilitarian agent} maximises lives saved.
Each persona is a native-language system prompt assembled from a header, one
sentence per WVS dimension with data, and a moral-reasoning closing line.

\noindent WVS grounding ensures empirically measured within-country variation rather
than stereotypes~\citep{deshpande2023toxicity}. Concrete persona examples and the
WVS-to-trolley dimension linkage are detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:personas}.

\subsection{Batched Evaluation and Logit Extraction}
\label{sec:method:eval}

All $N+1=5$ input sequences (base + 4 personas) are evaluated in a \emph{single}
batched forward pass:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_{\text{base}},\; \mathbf{z}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{z}_N
  = f_\theta\!\left(\mathbf{x};\; \varnothing,\; \mathbf{s}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_N\right).
  \label{eq:batch}
\end{equation}
Per-category logit temperatures $T_{\text{cat}}$ scale logits before computing
decision gaps. We use $T_{\text{cat}}=4.0$ (Species), $3.5$ (Gender), and $1.5$
for Age, Fitness, Social Value, and Utilitarianism (Table~\ref{tab:categories});
the same values appear in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}.
\begin{align}
  \delta_i &= \frac{z_{i,b} - z_{i,a}}{T_{\text{cat}}}, \quad
  \delta_{\text{base}} = \frac{z_{\text{base},b} - z_{\text{base},a}}{T_{\text{cat}}}, \quad
  \bar{\delta} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \delta_i \quad \text{(consensus)},
  \label{eq:consensus} \\
  r_i &= \delta_i - \delta_{\text{base}} \quad \text{(per-agent reward)}.
  \label{eq:reward}
\end{align}
Here $\delta_{\text{base}}$ is the decision gap of the base prompt (no persona).
The reward $r_i$ measures how strongly persona $i$ shifts the model \emph{away from
the culturally-neutral baseline}: a large $|r_i|$ indicates that the persona has
identified a direction of moral preference the vanilla model does not exhibit.
(Note: the scalar $r$ introduced later in Eq.~\ref{eq:dpbr_rel} denotes the
bootstrap reliability weight, not a reward.)
Inter-persona sample variance is logged as a diagnostic but \textbf{does not gate}
importance sampling in the released implementation (no $\tau$ threshold on
inter-persona variance).

\subsection{Positional Debiasing}
\label{sec:method:debias}

To cancel recency bias~\citep{liu2024lost}, each scenario is evaluated twice: once
in the original ordering, once with \emph{both} choice labels (A $\leftrightarrow$
B) and group labels (Group~A $\leftrightarrow$ Group~B) swapped in the native
language. The debiased decision gap for each agent is
$\delta_i = (\delta_i^{(\text{orig})} - \delta_i^{(\text{swap})})/2$; the base
prompt yields $\delta_{\text{base}}$ under the same rule.

\subsection{Prospect-Theory IS with Dual-Pass Reliability}
\label{sec:method:dpbr}

SWA-DPBR runs two \emph{independent} importance-sampling passes on every scenario.
Each pass draws $K_{\text{half}}=K/2=64$ perturbations
$\epsilon_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$; $\sigma$ defaults to $0.3$ but is
lifted to at least the empirical standard deviation of persona gaps when that
is larger. Candidates are
$\tilde{\delta}_k = \bar{\delta} + \epsilon_k$.

\paragraph{Per-persona gains and collective utility.}
For candidate $\tilde{\delta}_k = \bar{\delta} + \epsilon_k$, define
\emph{scalar gains} in logit-gap units that measure how much moving the
consensus toward $\tilde{\delta}_k$ reduces $L^1$ distance to each persona
relative to the base model:
\begin{align}
  g_{i,k} &= |\delta_{\text{base}} - \delta_i| - |\tilde{\delta}_k - \delta_i|,
  \label{eq:g_per_agent} \\
  g_{\text{cons},k} &= |\delta_{\text{base}} - \bar{\delta}| - |\tilde{\delta}_k - \bar{\delta}|.
  \label{eq:g_cons}
\end{align}
Large $g_{i,k}$ means $\tilde{\delta}_k$ is closer than $\delta_{\text{base}}$
to persona $i$'s debiased gap; $g_{\text{cons},k}$ is the same idea with the
persona consensus $\bar{\delta}$ as the reference. Prospect Theory is applied
\emph{per persona before averaging}, preserving loss aversion at the social
level~\citep{kahneman1979prospect}:
\begin{equation}
  U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon_k)
  = (1 - \lambda_{\text{coop}})\,\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
      v\!\left(\frac{g_{i,k}}{\sigma}\right)
  \;+\; \lambda_{\text{coop}}\,
      v\!\left(\frac{g_{\text{cons},k}}{\sigma}\right),
  \label{eq:util_total}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\text{coop}}=0.7$. Dividing by $\sigma$ makes the PT input
dimensionless and comparable across moral dimensions (as in the released controller).
The per-agent offsets $r_i = \delta_i - \delta_{\text{base}}$ from
Eq.~\ref{eq:reward} remain useful diagnostics but \textbf{do not} enter
$U_{\text{total}}$ in this implementation.
\begin{equation}
  v(x) = \begin{cases}
    x^{\alpha} & x \geq 0,\\
    -\kappa\,|x|^{\beta} & x < 0.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:prospect}
\end{equation}
We use $\alpha=\beta=0.88$ and $\kappa=2.25$~\citep{kahneman1979prospect}.
There is \textbf{no} separate quadratic penalty on $\epsilon_k$ in the
released utility: the Gaussian proposal $\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ already
limits step size, and an explicit KL-on-$\epsilon$ term is omitted (see
Appendix~\ref{app:dpbr_theory}).

\paragraph{Per-pass IS update and ESS guard.}
For each pass $m\in\{1,2\}$ we form softmax weights over its own $K_{\text{half}}$
samples,
\begin{equation}
  w_k^{(m)} = \frac{\exp\!\bigl(U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon_k)/\eta\bigr)}
             {\sum_{k'}\exp\!\bigl(U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon_{k'})/\eta\bigr)},
  \qquad
  \delta^{\star}_m = \sum_{k=1}^{K_{\text{half}}} w_k^{(m)}\,\epsilon_k,
  \label{eq:is_pass}
\end{equation}
with softmax temperature $\eta = 0.5$ (field \texttt{temperature} /
\texttt{mppi\_temperature} in \texttt{SWAConfig}; lower $\eta$ sharpens
the weight distribution; distinct from $T_{\text{dec}}$ scaling the final
sigmoid, though we also set $T_{\text{dec}}=0.5$).
Let $N_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(m)} = 1 / \sum_{k=1}^{K_{\text{half}}} (w_k^{(m)})^2$
(Kish effective sample size for normalised weights) and define the
\textbf{normalised} ratio $\widehat{\mathrm{ess}}^{(m)} =
N_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(m)}/K_{\text{half}} = 1 / (K_{\text{half}}
\sum_k (w_k^{(m)})^2)$.
If $\widehat{\mathrm{ess}}^{(m)} < \rho_{\mathrm{eff}}$ we set $\delta^{\star}_m \leftarrow 0$
(binary guard; default $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}=0.1$).

\paragraph{Bootstrap reliability and anchor blend.}
Define bootstrap variance $v_{\mathrm{b}} = (\delta^{\star}_1-\delta^{\star}_2)^2$
and soft reliability $r = \exp(-v_{\mathrm{b}}/s)$ with scale $s{=}0.04$
(\texttt{VAR\_SCALE}). The merged IS offset is
\begin{equation}
  \delta^{\star}_{\mathrm{IS}} \;=\; r \cdot \frac{\delta^{\star}_1 + \delta^{\star}_2}{2}.
  \label{eq:dpbr_rel}
\end{equation}
Large disagreement between passes shrinks $r$ and pulls the correction toward zero
without a hard gate. Finally, with $\mathrm{ess}_{\min}=\min(\widehat{\mathrm{ess}}^{(1)},
\widehat{\mathrm{ess}}^{(2)})$ and lower clip $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}$, set
$\alpha_{\mathrm{reg}} = \mathrm{clip}(\mathrm{ess}_{\min},\, \rho_{\mathrm{eff}},\, 1)$
and form the micro correction before the hierarchical prior,
\begin{equation}
  \delta_{\mathrm{micro}} = \alpha_{\mathrm{reg}}\,\bar{\delta}
    + (1-\alpha_{\mathrm{reg}})\,\delta_{\text{base}}
    + \delta^{\star}_{\mathrm{IS}},
  \label{eq:ess_anchor}
\end{equation}
where $\delta_{\text{base}}$ is the symmetric swap average for the base prompt
(\S\ref{sec:method:debias}).
When the ESS-anchor blend is \emph{enabled} (default in the released code), Eq.~\ref{eq:ess_anchor}
applies as written.
When it is \emph{disabled}, the implementation sets $\delta_{\mathrm{micro}}=\bar{\delta}+\delta^{\star}_{\mathrm{IS}}$
only (no interpolation toward $\delta_{\text{base}}$).
Geometrically, $\alpha_{\mathrm{reg}}=\mathrm{clip}(\mathrm{ess}_{\min},\,\rho_{\mathrm{eff}},\,1)$
interpolates between the persona consensus $\bar{\delta}$ and the debiased base
$\delta_{\text{base}}$: when both IS passes are diffuse ($\widehat{\mathrm{ess}}^{(m)}\!\approx\!1$),
$\alpha_{\mathrm{reg}}\approx 1$ and the consensus term dominates; when either pass
collapses, $\alpha_{\mathrm{reg}}$ approaches $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}$ and the update
leans toward~$\delta_{\text{base}}$.
All MultiTP numbers in this paper use the \emph{enabled} path unless noted.
In practice, $\alpha_{\mathrm{reg}}$ falls below $0.5$ on fewer than 8\% of
scenarios (indicating that the ESS guard rarely triggers aggressive anchoring);
the median $\alpha_{\mathrm{reg}}$ across the 20-country panel is $0.92$,
confirming that the persona consensus dominates under typical conditions.

\subsection{Hierarchical country prior}
\label{sec:method:prior}

After $\delta_{\mathrm{micro}}$, a scalar \emph{country-level} smoother runs per
ISO-3 key. Let $t\in\{0,1,\ldots\}$ index scenarios in processing order for country
$c$, and maintain an EMA statistic $\delta_{\mathrm{country}}$:
\begin{equation}
  \delta_{\mathrm{country}} \leftarrow (1-\beta_{\mathrm{ema}})\,\delta_{\mathrm{country}}
    + \beta_{\mathrm{ema}}\,\delta_{\mathrm{micro}},
  \label{eq:prior_ema}
\end{equation}
with $\beta_{\mathrm{ema}}=0.1$, updated after each scenario.
Let $\alpha_h(t)$ be an annealed blend weight,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_h(t) = \begin{cases}
    0, & t < N_{\mathrm{warm}}, \\[2pt]
    1 - \exp\!\bigl(-(t - N_{\mathrm{warm}})/\tau\bigr), & t \ge N_{\mathrm{warm}},
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:prior_alpha}
\end{equation}
with $N_{\mathrm{warm}}=50$ and $\tau=100$. Then
\begin{equation}
  \delta_{\mathrm{final}} = \alpha_h(t)\,\delta_{\mathrm{country}}
    + \bigl(1-\alpha_h(t)\bigr)\,\delta_{\mathrm{micro}}.
  \label{eq:prior_final}
\end{equation}
For $t<N_{\mathrm{warm}}$ the prior is inactive ($\delta_{\mathrm{final}}=\delta_{\mathrm{micro}}$);
thereafter the blend gradually shifts mass onto the EMA of past micro corrections.
At $t{=}150$ (a typical country pool size), $\alpha_h\approx 0.63$; the prior
contributes moderate smoothing without overriding per-scenario corrections.
Switching the prior off entirely (fixing $\alpha_h{=}0$) on the USA ablation slice
degrades JSD by ${\approx}\,3\%$ relative to the full configuration-a small but
consistent contribution that stabilises noisy IS updates in the tail of the
scenario pool.

The final spare probability is
$p_{\text{spare}} = \sigma(\delta_{\mathrm{final}} / T_{\text{dec}})$ with
$T_{\text{dec}}=0.5$.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:swa} summarises the full procedure; numerical values for all
symbols appear in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams} (Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{SWA-DPBR Inference for Scenario $\mathbf{x}$ in Country $c$}
\label{alg:swa}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Frozen LLM $f_\theta$; personas $\{\mathbf{s}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$;
         hyperparameters $K_{\text{half}},\sigma,\lambda_{\text{coop}},\eta,T_{\text{dec}},s$
\Ensure $p_{\text{spare}} \in [0,1]$
\For{$\text{order} \in \{\text{orig}, \text{swap}\}$}
  \State Batched forward: evaluate $f_\theta$ with base + $N$ personas
  \State Extract logit pairs; compute $\delta_i^{(\text{ord})}$ \hfill\Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:batch}}
\EndFor
\State Average: $\delta_i \leftarrow (\delta_i^{(\text{orig})} - \delta_i^{(\text{swap})})/2$ \hfill\Comment{\S\ref{sec:method:debias}}
\State Compute consensus $\bar{\delta}$, rewards $r_i$ \hfill\Comment{Eqs.~\ref{eq:consensus}--\ref{eq:reward}}
\For{$m \in \{1,2\}$}
  \For{$k = 1$ \textbf{to} $K_{\text{half}}$}
    \State Sample $\epsilon_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$; compute $U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon_k)$ \hfill\Comment{Eqs.~\ref{eq:g_per_agent}--\ref{eq:util_total}}
  \EndFor
  \State $\delta^{\star}_m \leftarrow \sum_k w_k^{(m)} \epsilon_k$ or $0$ if $\widehat{\mathrm{ess}}^{(m)}<\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}$ \hfill\Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:is_pass}}
\EndFor
\State $r \leftarrow \exp\bigl(-(\delta^{\star}_1-\delta^{\star}_2)^2/s\bigr)$;
       $\delta^{\star}_{\mathrm{IS}} \leftarrow r(\delta^{\star}_1+\delta^{\star}_2)/2$ \hfill\Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:dpbr_rel}}
\State $\delta_{\mathrm{micro}} \leftarrow$ blend per Eq.~\ref{eq:ess_anchor} \textbf{or} $\bar{\delta}+\delta^{\star}_{\mathrm{IS}}$ if ESS-anchor off \hfill\Comment{see \S\ref{sec:method:dpbr}}
\State $\delta_{\text{final}} \leftarrow$ country prior via Eqs.~\ref{eq:prior_ema}--\ref{eq:prior_final}
\State \Return $p_{\text{spare}} = \sigma(\delta_{\text{final}} / T_{\text{dec}})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

We evaluated \textbf{28 model--method combinations} spanning 12 architectures from 270M

design ensures that the moral reasoning is grounded in the target language's

The released configuration was selected from 25 method variants evaluated on a
fixed three-backbone, five-country prototyping panel (Qwen2.5-7B, Gemma-2-9B,

blending, entropy-based $\sigma$, Nesterov-style IS, category-coherence
regularisers, per-dimension priors, contrastive personas, and related knobs.

reliability was selected because it materially lowered IS flip rates versus
all other top-cluster methods while providing an interpretable per-scenario
uncertainty signal (Appendix~\ref{app:engineering}).

\emph{directional}, country-conditioned corrections; a controlled head-to-head on
the same MultiTP grid is important future work.

$r$} and \textbf{JSD} remain useful diagnostics and appear in the supplementary
comparison CSVs; the main table quotes mean MIS, vanilla$\to$DPBR macro relative
gain, country win rate (MIS lower than vanilla), and mean~$r$.

\subsection{Training-Free Steering Closes 19--24\% of the Cultural Gap}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

The results point to three connected insights about when and why inference-time cultural steering works.
The first is that \emph{within-country disagreement is the steering signal, not noise}. SWA-DPBR is deliberately lightweight-no gradients, no reward models, no activation hooks-and what makes it effective is that culturally grounded personas disagree in informative ways. When personas largely agree, the vanilla model is already close to the cultural target and corrections stay small; when they diverge, the PT--IS stage has room to move, and the dual-pass reliability gate ensures it only moves when both Monte Carlo estimates point the same direction. The result is a self-regulating correction that is aggressive where the model is miscalibrated and conservative where it is not.
The second insight is that \emph{failure tracks architecture, not geography}. A method that fails systematically on non-Western countries would be worse than useless, but per-country grids (\S\ref{sec:percountry_main}) show that SWA-DPBR's residual trouble spots correlate with collapsed decision-logit entropy-an architectural property of specific backbones-rather than with any continent or cultural region. The clearest example is Qwen2.5-32B, where decision entropy drops to $H{=}0.521$ nats (Table~\ref{tab:robustness_summary}; Appendix~\ref{app:adaptive_sigma}), causing IS perturbations to land off-manifold regardless of the target country. This architectural diagnosis also explains why the method scales across families: any backbone with a well-conditioned decision surface can benefit, regardless of parameter count.
The third insight is that \emph{the Prospect-Theory kernel earns its complexity}. One might expect that simple persona consensus suffices, but Table~\ref{tab:ablation} shows otherwise: dropping PT--IS (Row~1) degrades JSD by 16\% and Pearson~$r$ by 0.074, and the robustness suite (Table~\ref{tab:robustness_summary}) confirms that deterministic shifts and WVS-linear kernels consistently underperform the cooperative PT utility of Eq.~\ref{eq:util_total}. The nonlinear gain/loss asymmetry ($\kappa{=}2.25$) is load-bearing because it penalises corrections that \emph{worsen} alignment with any persona more heavily than it rewards equal-sized improvements-a natural fit for the ``do no harm'' desideratum in moral steering.

These three insights also delineate where the method reaches its limits. Per-dimension analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:perdim}) reveals that the hardest dimensions are Utilitarianism (Llama-3.3-70B predicts ${\approx}\,25\%$ where humans range 68--80\%, a 50-pp gap) and Species (smaller models stuck near 50\% where humans strongly prefer sparing humans at 77--94\%). SWA-DPBR shrinks aggregate MIS but cannot close gaps this large because the base logits offer no directional gradient to exploit. A complementary failure mode is diminishing returns: when vanilla MIS is already low for a (model, country) pair, the IS correction can overshoot-Phi-4 on Brazil ($0.274 \to 0.299$) and Iran ($0.397 \to 0.530$) are the clearest examples, illustrating that inference-time steering requires headroom in the base logits.

On the practical side, mean latency is 730\,ms per scenario versus 160\,ms for vanilla on Qwen2.5-72B / H100 (Appendix~\ref{app:trigger}), a 4.6$\times$ overhead dominated by the batched multi-agent forward pass (the IS loop operates on cached scalars and contributes $<$1\,ms). This overhead is acceptable for offline evaluation and batch moral auditing; for real-time deployment, the persona forward pass can be parallelised or amortised across scenarios sharing the same country context.

\textbf{Limitations.}
The caveats that most directly bound generalisation are as follows. Core hyperparameters ($\sigma$, $\lambda_{\text{coop}}$, $\eta$, $T_{\text{dec}}$, $T_{\text{cat}}$, reliability scale $s$) were selected on 200 GPT-4-generated dilemmas with author proxy labels and transfer-checked on 50 MultiTP scenarios; the temperature and dataset sweeps in Appendices~\ref{app:temp_sensitivity}--\ref{app:dataset_sens} are our main evidence that these values carry over, and the PT parameters $(\alpha,\beta,\kappa)$ follow classical monetary risk literature~\citep{kahneman1979prospect} as smooth gain/loss shaping on logit-gap coordinates rather than a normative claim about moral axioms. We optimise against country-level AMCE vectors from Moral Machine / MultiTP crowdsourcing~\citep{awad2018moral,jin2025multitp} without a human study of perceived cultural appropriateness~\citep{atari2023humans}, so the reader should treat our numbers as alignment to a survey statistic, not a proof of legitimacy. Per-dimension analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:perdim}) shows that Species and Utilitarianism are consistently the hardest dimensions, but a full per-dimension \emph{improvement} breakdown remains an open gap. SWA-DPBR also requires decision-token logits, limiting deployment on text-only hosted APIs. All headline results use a single random seed (42); multi-seed confidence intervals are not reported, though three forms of evidence partially mitigate this: the dual-pass bootstrap $r$ provides a per-scenario variance proxy (Eq.~\ref{eq:dpbr_rel}), the robustness suite (Table~\ref{tab:robustness_summary}) shows JSD spans within $\pm 0.004$, and the 25-method engineering comparison (Appendix~\ref{app:engineering}) confirms that the top cluster is separated by only 0.003 MIS. Nonetheless, multi-seed CIs remain a priority for future work; additional limitations are in Appendix~\ref{app:limitations}.

\textbf{Broader impact and safeguards.}
SWA-DPBR sits in a tension familiar from pluralistic alignment work~\citep{sorensen2024roadmap}: it can make models more responsive to documented cross-national preference heterogeneity without retraining, yet it can also bake in harmful majorities or survey stereotypes if deployed naively. We suggest four safeguards: governance ensuring SWA-DPBR does not override safety-critical guardrails and that domain experts review target cultures; periodic refresh of Wave~7-grounded personas as newer WVS data appear; audits of per-dimension AMCE shifts to catch entrenched bias; and optional clipping or penalty terms on sensitive dimensions inside $U_{\text{total}}$ to turn post-hoc review into decoding-time constraints. Survey-based personas add a measure of empirical accountability relative to ad-hoc author prompts, but they do not remove the risk of culturally legitimised harm.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

LLMs need not be retrained to respect moral diversity.
SWA-DPBR demonstrates that a frozen model's moral judgments can be steered toward
country-specific human preferences at inference time, using only survey-grounded
persona disagreement converted into bounded logit corrections via dual-pass
Prospect-Theory importance sampling
(Eqs.~\ref{eq:dpbr_rel}--\ref{eq:ess_anchor}).
The method reduces misalignment by 19--24\% across twenty countries without
touching a single weight.
Its limits are mechanical, not cultural: collapsed decision-logit entropy breaks
importance sampling regardless of geography.
The path forward is richer proposal adaptation, per-dimension outcome analysis,
extension to non-binary moral decisions, updated survey waves, and decoding-time
safeguards-steps toward the pluralistic alignment
vision~\citep{sorensen2024roadmap} with practical, deployable tools.

\section{AMCE Estimation Details}
\label{app:amce}

\paragraph{Binary dimensions.} For Species, Gender, Age, Fitness, and Social Value,
each scenario presents a forced choice between two character groups differing on
exactly one dimension. The AMCE reduces to the empirical mean of
$p_{\text{spare}}(\mathbf{x})$ across all scenarios in that dimension
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mpr}), consistent with MultiTP conventions~\citep{jin2025multitp}.

\paragraph{Utilitarianism dimension.} Utilitarianism scenarios vary the \emph{number}
of lives on each side rather than character identity. Since the count difference
$n_{\text{diff}} = n_{\text{pref}} - n_{\text{non-pref}}$ varies across scenarios
(typically 1--3), a simple mean would conflate signal strength with preference
direction. We therefore fit an OLS regression
$p_{\text{spare}} = a + b \cdot n_{\text{diff}}$ and evaluate at the mean observed
$n_{\text{diff}}$ in the data. This avoids underestimation: evaluating at
$n_{\text{diff}}=1$ would undercount the utilitarian signal when typical scenarios
have $n_{\text{diff}} \geq 2$. Scenarios with $n_{\text{diff}}=0$ (equal group sizes)
are excluded as non-discriminative.

\paragraph{Human AMCE scale.} The MultiTP ground-truth AMCEs in
\texttt{country\_specific\_ACME.csv} are on a $[-1, 1]$ scale. We convert to
$[0, 100]$ via $(1 + \text{AMCE}_{\text{raw}}) / 2 \times 100$, where 50\%
represents no preference and 100\% represents maximal preference for the
``preferred'' group. Model AMCEs are computed on the same scale.

\paragraph{Consistency.} \citet{awad2018moral} show that marginal averages are
unbiased estimators under the balanced randomisation design of the Moral Machine,
making our estimation valid and reproducible.

\subsection{WVS Data Processing Pipeline}
\label{app:wvs_pipeline}

We process WVS Wave~7 individual-level microdata following the 10-variable
cultural-value scheme of Greco et al.\ (2025):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Variable extraction:} For each respondent, we extract 10 value
    dimensions from WVS items (Table~\ref{tab:wvs_thresholds}). The ``inverted''
    WVS-7 CSV (suffix P) pre-flips Likert items so higher values consistently
    indicate the positive pole. Items without the P suffix (Q152, Q153,
    Q177--Q182) use original WVS coding.
  \item \textbf{Age cohort assignment:} Using birth year (Q261) and survey year
    (A\_YEAR), we compute age and assign to: \emph{young} ($<$36),
    \emph{middle} (36--55), \emph{older} ($>$55). Respondents with birth year
    before 1900 or after 2010, or survey year before 2015, are excluded.
    Negative WVS codes ($-1, -2, -4, -5$: refusal / don't know) are dropped;
    zero is retained for binary 0/1 items.
  \item \textbf{Cohort aggregation:} For each country and age cohort, we compute
    the mean of each dimension across all valid respondents.
  \item \textbf{Normalisation and descriptor mapping:} Raw dimension means are
    normalised to $[0,1]$ via $(\text{value}-\text{lo})/(\text{hi}-\text{lo})$
    where $(\text{lo},\text{hi})$ is the WVS scale range, then directionally
    flipped so higher $=$ positive pole. Four-level descriptors are assigned
    by quartile cuts: $\geq 0.75$ (strong positive), $\geq 0.50$, $\geq 0.25$,
    $< 0.25$ (strong negative). See Table~\ref{tab:wvs_thresholds}.
  \item \textbf{Fallback:} For countries with insufficient WVS coverage (e.g.,
    Saudi Arabia), we use manually authored native-language personas grounded in
    area-studies literature, covering the same demographic structure (3 age
    cohorts + 1 utilitarian).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{WVS-to-Trolley Dimension Linkage}
\label{app:wvs_linkage}

The linkage between WVS value dimensions and trolley moral dimensions operates
through two mechanisms.

\emph{Direct thematic overlap:} WVS \emph{gender equality} maps onto the
Gender dimension (societies scoring higher show weaker female-sparing
preferences~\citep{awad2018moral}); \emph{religiosity} correlates with
Species (human exceptionalism via religious anthropocentrism);
\emph{moral acceptability} relates to tolerance of trade-offs in Fitness and
Social Value dimensions.

\emph{Indirect modulation:} Dimensions like \emph{social trust},
\emph{child-rearing values}, and \emph{materialism orientation} modulate the
moral \emph{frame}; e.g., post-materialist personas favour utilitarian calculi,
while obedience-oriented child-rearing values weight hierarchical social roles
(affecting Age and Social Value).
Crucially, SWA-DPBR does not require exact causal mapping: the 10-dimensional
ensemble generates diversity in logit-space gaps, and the IS stage selects the
correction balancing collective utility. A rigorous per-dimension causal analysis
(e.g., regressing WVS attributes against AMCE shifts) would further strengthen
this argument.

\section{PT--IS, Sampling, and Dual-Pass Reliability}
\label{app:dpbr_theory}

\subsection{Utility shape and the omitted explicit KL-on-$\epsilon$ term}

The scalar decision gap $\delta$ acts as a one-dimensional state and the
perturbations $\epsilon_k$ as control inputs at a single horizon step. Under
the path-integral / free-energy view~\citep{williams2017mppi}, softmax
weights $w_k \propto \exp(U_{\text{total}}/\eta)$ can be read as targeting a
Boltzmann distribution over candidate perturbations. \textbf{Our released code
does not add} a separate quadratic penalty
$\propto \epsilon_k^2/(2\sigma^2)$ to $U_{\text{total}}$ in
Eq.~\ref{eq:util_total}: the released controller matches it exactly, and
the Gaussian proposal $\epsilon_k\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ already limits
how far $\tilde{\delta}_k$ can move from $\bar{\delta}$. An optional explicit
KL-on-control term remains a straightforward extension if future work needs
sharper tail control on $|\epsilon_k|$.

\subsection{Comparison with Simpler Alternatives}

Temperature scaling uniformly stretches the logit gap without accounting for
direction or magnitude of persona rewards. A fixed margin offset ignores the
utility landscape, treating all perturbations as equally valuable. PT-weighted IS performs an
\emph{adaptive, scenario-specific} correction via nonlinear importance weighting.
Auxiliary runs with deterministic consensus-only shifts and WVS-linear reward kernels
underperform the Prospect-Theory kernel on the same pipeline; we omit utility-shape
rows from Table~\ref{tab:ablation} for clarity.

\subsection{Why split $K$ into two passes?}

Self-normalised IS is biased at finite sample size; two independent halves with
the \emph{same} total budget trade per-pass variance for a direct probe of
sampling instability. The reliability map $r=\exp(-(\delta^\star_1-\delta^\star_2)^2/s)$
is intentionally smooth-unlike a hard veto it preserves a single code path and
down-weights only when the two estimates contradict each other strongly. It should
be read as a variance heuristic, not as a statistical guarantee of unbiasedness;
formal finite-$K$ bounds would require stronger assumptions on the logit landscape
than we are willing to claim here.

\subsection{Positional Debiasing: Implementation Detail}
\label{app:debias_impl}

The two-pass debiasing of \S\ref{sec:method:debias} is implemented as a
text-level rewrite of the scenario \emph{after} native-language framing. To
avoid the trivial bug of double-substitution (replacing
\textsc{LEFT}~$\to$~\textsc{RIGHT} immediately followed by
\textsc{RIGHT}~$\to$~\textsc{LEFT} would leave every label as
\textsc{LEFT}), we use a placeholder-based three-step swap, applied
independently for the lane-label pair and the group-label pair:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Replace every occurrence of the left label with a unique placeholder
    token (a non-printing sentinel).
  \item Replace every occurrence of the right label with the left label.
  \item Replace the placeholder with the right label.
\end{enumerate}
The same procedure is applied to Group~A $\leftrightarrow$ Group~B in the
native script. Logit gaps $(\delta_{\text{base}},\delta_i)$ from the two orders
are combined \emph{before} any nonlinear step,
$\delta \leftarrow (\delta^{(\text{orig})}-\delta^{(\text{swap})})/2$,
matching the released controller (debias
first, then PT--IS and dual-pass merge). This guarantees cancellation of additive
positional bias in logit space; the final $p_{\text{spare}}$ applies
$\sigma(\cdot)$ only after that correction.

\section{Dataset Preprocessing and Sensitivity}
\label{app:dataset}
\label{app:dataset_sens}

\paragraph{Pipeline.}
Each MultiTP CSV is processed in five steps: (i) deduplication keeps only
the canonical paraphrase (\texttt{which\_paraphrase\,=\,0}); (ii) the
Utilitarianism \emph{quality filter} drops scenarios where the two groups
have identical size \emph{and} all characters belong to the
\emph{quality-attribute} role set $\{\textsc{Pregnant},\,\textsc{Woman},\,
\textsc{LargeWoman}\}$, which would otherwise inject a non-numerosity signal
into a dimension whose AMCE is by construction the count-difference effect;
(iii) per-category capping retains at most 80 scenarios per dimension;
(iv) under-represented categories are oversampled with replacement to a
minimum of 36 scenarios; and (v) the resulting pool is shuffled with a fixed
seed (42).

\paragraph{Reproducible left/right assignment.}
When the MultiTP \texttt{paraphrase\_choice} field does not unambiguously
determine which group is rendered on the left vs.\ right, we deterministically
fall back to a SHA-256 hash of the tuple
$(\textit{sub}_1,\textit{sub}_2,g_1,g_2)$ modulo $2$, so the same scenario
receives the same ordering across runs and machines. The fallback rate is
logged per country; for all 15 countries it stays below 5\%.

\section{Cross-Lingual Token Elicitation}
\label{app:token_elicitation}

\paragraph{Native-language prompting system.}
Each scenario is rendered entirely in the target country's native language.
The implementation includes full translations of: (i)~scenario framing and
context sentences (4 paraphrase variants per language), (ii)~all 22 character
types with singular/plural forms in each language, (iii)~lane labels and group
identifiers in native script, and (iv)~closing questions with
language-appropriate conjunctions and grammar.

The prompt frame wraps the scenario in a native-language instruction that
explicitly requests an English answer token. Each frame follows the pattern:
``[moral dilemma preamble in native language] \textbackslash n \{scenario\}
\textbackslash n [instruction to answer A or B in English]''.
This ensures moral reasoning occurs in the culturally native linguistic
frame while maintaining a consistent decision interface.

\paragraph{Token verification.}
Token IDs are verified per model at initialisation by encoding the uppercase
strings \texttt{"A"} and \texttt{"B"} with
\texttt{add\_special\_tokens=False} and asserting single-token mappings:
Qwen2.5: A=22397, B=11572; Llama-3.1: A=31266, B=9268.
Logits for these two positions are extracted from the last-position output
tensor; no generation sampling is performed. The model uses its built-in
\texttt{chat\_template} for correct role formatting across model families.

\section{Baseline Implementation Details}
\label{app:baselines}

\subsection{Activation Steering}
For each country, a steering vector is computed as the difference in mean
residual-stream activations from 20 culturally contrastive prompt pairs (e.g.,
``Answer as someone from [country] who values [WVS-high trait]'' vs.\ ``Answer
as someone with the opposite values''). Vectors are extracted from layer~32
(transformer midpoint) of Llama-3.1-70B and applied at inference time by adding
$\alpha \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{steer}}$ to the residual stream, with
$\alpha=1.5$ selected via the same synthetic validation set used for SWA-DPBR.

\paragraph{Sensitivity analysis and fairness considerations.}
Activation steering performance is known to be sensitive to (i)~the extraction
layer, (ii)~the scaling coefficient $\alpha$, and (iii)~the construction of
contrastive pairs~\citep{arditi2025refusal}. We conducted a limited sensitivity
check: extracting from layers 24, 32, and 40 with $\alpha \in \{0.5, 1.0, 1.5,
2.0, 3.0\}$, we found that layer~32 with $\alpha=1.5$ yielded the lowest mean
JSD (0.0877); other configurations ranged from 0.089 to 0.112. We note
that a more exhaustive search-varying contrastive prompt design, using
multiple layers simultaneously, or employing PCA-based direction
refinement-could improve activation steering performance. However, even the
best configuration performs below vanilla on this benchmark, suggesting a
fundamental mismatch between linear activation interventions and the
multi-dimensional moral calibration problem. The key structural advantage of
SWA-DPBR is that it operates \emph{per-scenario} with adaptive correction
magnitude, whereas activation steering applies a fixed direction uniformly
across all scenarios.

\subsection{ARGS and controlled decoding (relation to our work)}
ARGS~\citep{khanov2024args} and controlled decoding~\citep{mudgal2024controlled}
typically assume trained reward models; a cross-cultural deployment would require a
separate reward per target country. On binary forced-choice MultiTP, one can instead
define rewards as closed-form functionals of persona logit gaps; a Prospect-Theory
kernel aligned with Eq.~\ref{eq:util_total} is then the natural analogue of
importance-weighted PT--IS. We do not run the full ARGS decoding stack in our
experiments; Table~\ref{tab:robustness_summary} and \S\ref{sec:ablation} isolate the
kernel comparison on the shared SWA-DPBR pipeline.

\section{Extended Limitations}
\label{app:limitations}

\paragraph{Single seed and stochasticity.}
All main stochastic runs use one seed per model; each scenario runs two independent
IS passes of $K_{\text{half}}{=}64$ proposals (128 total), matching the released
pipeline. That dual-pass design provides an internal
reliability signal (Eq.~\ref{eq:dpbr_rel}) but \textbf{not} multi-seed confidence
intervals for macro MIS; we therefore report headline gains as point estimates and
treat seed-level CIs as a priority for future work.

\paragraph{Pearson $r$ in six dimensions.}
As noted in Section~\ref{sec:results}, the AMCE vector is short, so per-country
$r$ has large sampling error and small row-wise $r$ deltas are not necessarily
statistically separable; primary claims use MIS, with JSD as a secondary diagnostic.

\paragraph{WVS-to-trolley linkage.}
The mapping from WVS value dimensions to trolley moral dimensions follows the
indirect-modulation construction in Appendix~\ref{app:wvs_linkage}, not a fully
validated causal chain; we therefore avoid claims of the form ``WVS dimension $X$
caused AMCE shift $Y$.''

\paragraph{Inter-persona reward assumption.}
The reward $r_i = \delta_i - \delta_{\text{base}}$ assumes persona shifts approximate
human target directions. This is plausible when the base model represents a WEIRD-biased
prior, but if a persona's shift is orthogonal to the human target, the signal may
mislead. Per-dimension validation is needed.
Quantifying whether persona-consensus directions correlate with country-level human
AMCE vectors independently of the base model-and when corrections diverge from
targets-is also open; we do not report that correlation matrix here.

\paragraph{Geographic coverage, preprocessing, and survey vintage.}
We report 20 countries in the main MIS sweep (Table~\ref{tab:main_macro_summary}) out of
100+ MultiTP countries; results depend on the preprocessing pipeline in
Appendix~\ref{app:dataset_sens}.
WVS Wave~7 ($\sim$2017--2022) is a fixed historical slice; persona quality is
weakest where coverage is sparse (e.g., Saudi Arabia, Brazil), as reflected in
per-country tables.
No low-resource languages (Amharic, Khmer, Yoruba) appear in the panel;
cross-lingual generalisation to such settings remains untested.

\paragraph{Binary format.}
The logit-difference formulation requires generalisation to softmax for multi-option
settings.

\paragraph{Language--culture conflation.}
One language per country may conflate linguistic and cultural effects.

\paragraph{Model quantisation.}
4-bit/Int8 quantisation may introduce logit artefacts affecting IS stability.

\paragraph{WVS temporal staleness.}
Wave~7 ($\sim$2017--2022) data may not reflect current preferences in rapidly
evolving societies.

\paragraph{Qwen2.5-32B failure mode.}
Concentrated logit distributions ($H{=}0.521$ nats) cause IS perturbations to
land off-manifold and yield near-uniform importance weights. Appendix~\ref{app:adaptive_sigma}
shows that an entropy-aware $\sigma$ correctly diagnoses the cause but cannot
fix it on its own, because all 8 evaluated countries share the same low
entropy and thus cannot be discriminated by a per-scenario entropy signal.
A complete remedy requires richer jointly adaptive $\sigma$ (and related proposal
knobs) per country and is left to future work.

The top cluster (ranks 1--7) is separated by only 0.0026 MIS, but DPBR was
selected over the marginally-better EXP-23 for two reasons:
(i)~DPBR materially \textbf{lowers flip rate} (the fraction of scenarios where IS
reverses the consensus decision) compared to all other top-cluster methods,
improving deployment stability; and
(ii)~the dual-pass bootstrap provides an \textbf{interpretable uncertainty signal}

The base SWA-PTIS (rank~18) shows the value added by the hierarchical
prior and dual-pass stages: a 7\% relative MIS improvement.
Contrastive persona decoding (rank~25) \emph{hurts} alignment, validating the
design choice discussed in \S\ref{sec:related} to use direct WVS personas
rather than contrastive amplification.
